Legal Notice — Real Property Law
Your timeshare is not a membership or travel club. It is recorded real property — subject to the same laws as a house or land parcel. That recording triggers the full weight of real property law and does not disappear when you do.
Why Your Timeshare Is Legally Real Property

Most owners are never told this at the point of sale. The developer sold you a deeded interest — your name was recorded with the county clerk and recorder the same way a home purchase is recorded.

State Timeshare Act — Real Property ClassificationDeeded Ownership

Timeshare estates constitute an ownership interest in real property and are subject to all laws governing real property in the state where the resort is located. Your ownership is expressly classified as real property — not a license, not a right-to-use, not a club membership.

What this means: Your timeshare appears on property tax rolls, is part of your legal estate, and cannot be abandoned without a formal legal transfer of title.
Property Taxes — The Obligation That Never Stops

Because your ownership is real property, the resort collects and remits property taxes on your behalf through your maintenance fee. You are a legally assessed property taxpayer in the county where the resort is located — whether you use the unit or not.

State Property Tax Law — Annual AssessmentC.R.S. §39-1-102 / State Equivalent

All real property is subject to annual valuation and taxation. There are no exceptions for timeshare interests — if your name is on a deed, you are a taxable property owner.

Property Tax Lien — Automatic AttachmentC.R.S. §39-1-107 / State Equivalent

A property tax lien attaches to real property by operation of law on January 1st of each tax year — automatically, without any action by the county or the resort. This lien is a government-priority obligation: it cannot be discharged in bankruptcy and it survives the death of the owner.

What this means: If you pass away while holding this ownership, the tax lien does not disappear. It becomes a claim against your estate that must be resolved before your heirs receive anything.
Maintenance Fees — A Lien on Real Property

Your annual maintenance obligation is not simply a bill you can ignore. It is a covenant that runs with the land — permanently attached to the property and enforceable against every future owner, including your heirs.

Common Interest Ownership Act — HOA Lien RightsC.R.S. §38-33.3-316 / State Equivalent

A homeowners association has the statutory right to record a lien against an owner's real property interest for any unpaid assessments, including maintenance fees. This lien attaches automatically upon non-payment, accrues interest and collection costs, and is enforceable through judicial foreclosure.

What this means: Every missed maintenance payment becomes a recorded lien on real property — the same legal instrument used to foreclose a mortgage. This lien survives death and attaches to your estate.

What Happens to Your Heirs
The Part Most Owners Never Anticipate

Unless this ownership is legally terminated before your passing, your next of kin will face every obligation below — simply because your name remains on a county deed.

1
Probate Obligation

The timeshare appears as a real property asset in your estate and must be formally addressed during probate. Your executor cannot ignore it. The resort will continue billing — growing at 8% annually — throughout probate, which averages 9 to 18 months.

State Probate Code / C.R.S. §15-12-805
2
Property Tax Liens Are Paid First

Any delinquent property taxes are priority liens against the estate. They must be satisfied before any assets can be distributed to your beneficiaries — meaning your children receive less, or nothing, until the lien is cleared.

State Property Tax Law / C.R.S. §39-11-101
3
The 9-Month Disclaimer Window

An heir who does not want this timeshare must formally disclaim it in writing within 9 months of the date of death. Miss that window and they legally inherit the ownership — along with all future maintenance fees, property taxes, and lien exposure — in perpetuity, for a property they may never use and cannot easily sell.

26 U.S.C. §2518 — Federal / C.R.S. §15-11-1201 — State
4
Foreclosure Against Your Estate

If the estate cannot pay accumulated liens and no heir accepts the property, the resort can initiate foreclosure proceedings. This can cloud title on other estate assets, delay distribution, and reduce what your family ultimately receives.

Common Interest Ownership Act / C.R.S. §38-33.3-316(8)
